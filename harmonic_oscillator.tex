\section{Information Theory and Erasure Action}

The fundamental bound on information erasure, derived from the Landauer principle and the energy-time uncertainty relation, is given by:
\begin{equation}
    b E \tau \le \pi \hbar
\end{equation}
where $b$ is the count of bits erased, $E$ is the erasure energy ($k_B T \ln 2$), and $\tau$ is the erasure time. This product defines the erasure action $A$. For a system in phase space, emerging from the potential and kinetic components of the Hamiltonian $H=V+T$, we define a conjugate pair of action ellipses as coordinate and momentum components:
\begin{equation}
    (b_q + b_p) E \tau \le A_q + A_p \le \pi \hbar
\end{equation}

\subsection{Ellipses vs. Rectangles}
We must distinguish between the bounding box (rectangle) and the actual action (elliptical area). Let $\Delta q$ and $\Delta p$ be the \textbf{full widths (diameters)} of the erasure region. The action area $A_q$ is defined by the ellipse with minor axis $\delta_q$ and major axis $\Delta p$:
\begin{equation}
    A_q = \frac{\pi}{4} \delta_q \Delta p \le \frac{\pi \hbar}{2}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Hierarchy of Uncertainty}
The erasure distance $\epsilon$ is the distance between the observable state $q$ and the unknowable microstate $\mu$. This distance is bounded by the action quantum width $\delta$, which is in turn bounded by system diameter $\Delta$:
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon_q = |\mu_q - q| \le \delta_q \le \Delta q
\end{equation}

\section{Dimensionless Normalization}

To facilitate simulation, we switch to dimensionless units using characteristic scales $q_0$ and $p_0$. We define $\mathcal{Q}$ and $\mathcal{P}$ as the \textbf{dimensionless radii} (semi-axes) of the system.

\subsection{Variable Definitions}
The diameters (full widths) are related to the dimensionless radii as follows:
\begin{align}
    \Delta q &= q_0 (2\mathcal{Q}) \\
    \Delta p &= p_0 (2\mathcal{P})
\end{align}

We define the characteristic scale such that 2 characteristic ellipses (one for each dimension) is:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\pi}{2}q_0 p_0 = \pi\hbar \implies q_0 p_0 = 2\hbar
\end{equation}

\subsection{Deriving the Dimensionless Bound}
Substituting the radius definitions into the action limit (Eq. 3):
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\pi}{4} q_0 |q_{?} - \mathbf{q}^*| p_0 (2\mathcal{P}) \le \frac{\pi \hbar}{2}
\end{equation}
Substituting $q_0 p_0 = 2\hbar$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\pi}{4} (2\hbar) |q_{?} - \mathbf{q}^*| (2\mathcal{P}) \le \frac{\pi \hbar}{2}
\end{equation}
Consolidating constants:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\pi \hbar}{2} |q_{?} - \mathbf{q}^*| (2\mathcal{P}) \le \frac{\pi \hbar}{2}
\end{equation}
Canceling the common term and isolating the distance:
\begin{equation}
    |q_{?} - \mathbf{q}^*| \le \frac{1}{2 \mathcal{P}}
\end{equation}
